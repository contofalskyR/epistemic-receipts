# Settling Curves: A Data Structure for How Facts Change Status

*The technical companion to ["Facts Have Histories"](#) — the schema, the two promotion engines, and what it takes to stop a language model from inventing history. All figures as of July 2026.*

---

The white paper behind Epistemic Receipts makes a single claim: a fact is not a verdict handed down once, but a chain of receipts accumulating over time — and the moment of consensus is a snapshot of an ongoing story, not its end. Semaglutide is that thesis compressed into one figure: a mouse study in 1996, a rat study in 2001, a first-in-human trial in 2010, an FDA approval for diabetes in 2017, an approval for weight management in 2021 — five receipts, thirty years, one continuous claim, which arrived in most people's field of view as a single event in 2023.

This post is about the machinery that holds such chains at scale. Epistemic Receipts is a Next.js application on Neon Postgres holding just over 1.6 million claims (1,625,084 at the July census) from 174 distinct sources, each claim traceable to a fetchable primary document. Its central data structure is the **settling curve**: a dated, sourced sequence of status transitions recording not whether a claim is true, but what its documented status is and how it moved there. What follows: the schema, why most of the corpus deliberately *doesn't* move, the deterministic waves that built about 224,000 multi-step curves, the LLM promoter whose most common output is a refusal, and the audit incidents that shaped all of it.

## The schema

A curve is a chain of `ClaimStatusHistory` rows attached to a claim. Each row records a transition `fromAxis → toAxis` over a small status vocabulary — `RECORDED`, `SETTLED`, `CONTESTED`, `OPEN`, `UNRESOLVABLE`, `REVERSED`, `ABANDONED` — with `fromAxis = null` marking the claim's entry into the record. The failure modes are first-class outcomes, not errors; a schema comment states the thesis outright: *this product measures fact-status, not truth.*

Each transition carries four things beyond the states themselves. `occurredAt` is the historical date the transition happened — the retraction date, the enactment date — never the ingest time; the day the system *learned* something is metadata about the system, not about the fact. `datePrecision` (`DAY | MONTH | QUARTER | YEAR`) keeps the system from performing precision it doesn't have: a paper dated only "2014" produces a year-precision transition, honestly labeled. `community` names who ratified the transition, from a five-value enum: `EXPERT_LITERATURE`, `INSTITUTIONAL`, `JUDICIAL`, `PUBLIC`, `MARKET`. A single claim's curve can cross communities — which is exactly what the white paper's smoking example demands: studies in the expert literature from the 1930s, the Surgeon General's institutional declaration in 1964, and the thirty-year gap between them *visible as structure* rather than buried in narrative. And `sourceId` points to the *marker artifact*: the specific document that caused the transition.

Two integrity details turned out to matter more than any feature. First, a uniqueness constraint on `(claimId, toAxis, occurredAt)` — added after a deduplication pass removed 191,000 redundant rows that idempotency conventions alone had failed to prevent. Second, deterministic row IDs for all bulk writers (`${claimId}-${toAxis}-${YYYY-MM-DD}`), so that re-running any wave is a no-op by construction rather than by hope.

## The corpus, and the problem of the 1.27 million

Claims arrive through ingest pipelines — about 179 ingest scripts accumulated over the project's life, feeding 174 distinct sources across seven categories. By volume: national parliaments and legislation (86 sources, 405,391 claims — Hungary, Argentina, the Czech Republic, Italy, and dozens more statute registries), science and medicine (26 sources, 488,285 claims, anchored by hundreds of thousands of OpenAlex papers and 26,624 CrossRef retractions), and US federal records (18 sources, 358,640 claims, dominated by NARA's declassified catalog and Voteview's congressional roll-calls back to 1789). Courts, international organizations, pharmaceutical regulation, and historical archives round out the registry.

Ingestion runs under one non-negotiable rule, written into the contributor docs after an early disaster I'll get to: **training-data recall is not a verifiable source.** Every record comes from a live API or a document that can be fetched and checked. Two more house rules: `humanReviewed` and `autoApproved` are separate flags that may never be conflated (a pipeline may not promote itself to human-verified), and pipeline results are verified against database counts, never against the script's own progress logs — counters lie in ways row counts don't.

Roughly 235,000 claims have multi-step curves. For a while, an internal metric treated the remainder — about 1.27 million single-step claims — as a promotion backlog. The audit that killed that metric is my favorite artifact in the repo.

The realization: **a curve of length 1 is usually complete.** An enacted statute is *born settled* — its baseline transition (`null → SETTLED` at enactment) is the entire honest arc, and there is no earlier dated event to add without fabricating one. A World Bank indicator is *born recorded* and should stay that way — the value is subject to revision, and auto-settling it would be epistemically sloppy. The fix was a code-level partition of all 186 Layer-1 pipeline tags (`lib/corpus-completeness.ts`): 120 born-settled, 47 born-recorded, 9 wave-1 promotable, 3 retraction-shaped, 5 conditional, and exactly 2 that need actual research. That reclassified 790,611 claims from "backlog" to "finished," with a one-line justification I'd defend anywhere: *a settling curve of length 1 is a real epistemic claim — nothing has moved yet. Faking motion would be the USPTO lesson in new clothes.*

Curve-building then splits by epistemic character, not by engineering convenience. The white paper calls the general strategy *semi-automated trajectory construction* — use the bulk layer to draft trajectories mechanically where possible, and spend human and model attention only where the record demands interpretation. In practice that means two engines.

## Engine one: deterministic waves

Where reality documents a transition, no intelligence is required — only care.

**Wave 1** handled records whose settling event is definitional: a certified roll-call vote is institutionally settled the day it enters the official record; an approved FDA label is the operative regulatory document from its effective date. One audited pass wrote 205,679 `RECORDED → SETTLED` transitions (113,318 historical roll-call votes, 84,931 FDA labels, plus parliamentary votes from several countries) as plain `INSERT … SELECT` statements. The discipline around it is the point: dry-run preflight by default with writes behind an explicit `--execute` flag; an `expectedEntryAxis` guard that skips any claim whose baseline isn't the shape the rule was written for; claims missing a source date skipped and *counted* — the skip tally is a first-class output; and bind-parameterized SQL only, a security-review invariant. Every promoted row is identifiable by its deterministic ID and reason text, which makes rollback a `DELETE` by reason rather than a restore from backup.

**Wave 2** produced the corpus's most instructive curves: retractions. The CrossRef registry contributes 26,624 retracted papers, initially ingested as single `null → REVERSED` rows at the retraction date. The canonical shape needs the birth too: `RECORDED` at publication, `REVERSED` at retraction — the receipt logic applied to a paper's death, which is what lets the retraction explorer trace *citation half-life*: how long, and how often, a paper kept being cited after it was formally withdrawn. But the original ingester had fetched publication dates from CrossRef and *discarded* them. The fix was a re-sweep that verified — against the live API — that each stored work really is the original paper, backfilled `metadata.originalPublished`, and then prepended publication rows under strict guards: the date must parse, must strictly precede the retraction date, or the claim is skipped, never guessed. 18,280 claims came out with the two-step curve; the residue where publisher metadata was defective was left as documented residue for slower review. This wave was also the single sanctioned exception to "never modify existing rows" — re-pointing the baseline's `fromAxis` to keep the chain coherent — and it required its own explicit consent flag.

## Engine two: the promoter that mostly says no

That leaves the one large corpus whose arcs require research rather than rules: academic papers. Whether anything documentable ever happened to a given paper — a retraction, a failed replication, an adjudicating meta-analysis, a consensus statement — cannot be derived from ingest metadata. So an LLM promoter loop works the OpenAlex queue (217,421 papers when it was pointed there), ordered by citation count so effort lands on claims someone will actually read.

Its prompt is mostly prohibitions. It checks four genres of dated adjudicating event (retraction or expression of concern; registered replication or major methodological critique; systematic review or meta-analysis; consensus shift into guidelines). It must emit **SKIP** when no specific, dated, citable follow-up exists — and the prompt states that SKIP is the expected outcome for most papers. A high citation count alone is explicitly not evidence of settling. Every proposed transition must carry an exact date with declared precision, a ratifying community, a written reason, and one URL the promoter has verified *resolves* — fetched, not assumed — or the transition is discarded. Attempts, promotions, and skips all land in an append-only ledger.

The division of labor is the design thesis in miniature: the deterministic engine is forbidden from inventing motion, and the probabilistic engine is rewarded for admitting stillness. Most of what an LLM contributes here is a defensible *no*.

## The audit trail includes the system itself

The rule about training-data recall exists because of Pipeline 5. Early on, a USPTO patents pipeline — built when no working API was available — let model memory stand in for the registry. The audit finding was unambiguous: one record, US4431740, carried the correct patent number but the title and inventors of a different patent entirely (US4237224, the Cohen–Boyer patent). All 182 records were retired: not deleted but set to `DEPRECATED` with the deprecation reason in metadata, excluded from default views, preserved for audit, and written up on a public corrections page.

That page (`/corrections`) has since accumulated the system's other self-findings, including a schema diagnostic that swept all 1,466,486 then-active claims and surfaced 4,897 records carrying status values written by historical ingesters that no documented vocabulary contained — resolved by explicit mapping during the migration to the current status axis, with the legacy columns preserved. The same instinct shows up in commit messages I'd hold up as a genre: *"zeitgeist data quality — word-boundary tagger, taxonomy rulings, honest denominators"*; *"legislation curve rail labels itself honestly as curated showcase."* Even the homepage statistics are database-derived rather than hand-written, after an audit flagged a hardcoded marketing number as off-brand for a provenance product. If the pitch is that every claim carries a receipt, the site's claims about itself have to carry them first.

## The honest imbalance

The white paper states the project's limitation plainly, and it belongs here too: nearly all of the 1.6 million claims are reference scaffolding — bulk-imported, machine-ingested, structurally sound, but not curated into a receipt-by-receipt narrative. The case studies that demonstrate the thesis — semaglutide, Korematsu, the tobacco record, Pluto's reclassification — live in hand-tagged seed collections, a few thousand claims out of 1.6 million. The machine-derived curves sit between those poles: real, documented, defensible transitions, but drawn from what registries record, not from editorial investigation.

That imbalance is the design's honest state, and also its argument. A chain of receipts cannot be produced by summarizing faster; it has to be assembled from the actual dated record, which is slower and more defensible than a verdict for exactly the same reason. The schema to hold trajectories at scale exists. Turning a bulk record into a fully-sourced case study is still manual labor, and still the bottleneck.

## What a transition corpus is for

A quarter-million dated status transitions are not just a browsing surface; they're a dataset about self-correction. Publication-to-retraction spans across 26,000+ papers. Survival analysis on contested findings: how long does a contest take to resolve, and does it differ by community or by the genre of evidence that finally moves status — do registered replications adjudicate faster than meta-analyses? A curated instance for cognitive science — about a hundred hand-documented trajectories through the replication crisis, with named curators and a right of reply for original authors — is in the works as a methods-first registry.

The open problems are the white paper's Future Work, in ascending difficulty. *Semi-automated trajectory construction* is furthest along — the waves and the promoter are its first implementation, with the human curator confirming rather than assembling from scratch. *Quiet reversals* are the hard case: most scientific reversal never produces a retraction notice — a finding just stops being cited, or a later meta-analysis contradicts it without ceremony. Detecting that means modeling citation trajectories over time, not checking a flag, and the pipeline for it (citation-trajectory screening → candidate flagging → LLM research → a human gate before any status changes) is deliberately the most gated thing in the system, because it's the first place a transition would rest on editorial judgment rather than a single adjudicating document. *Cross-domain linking* is the payoff case: a court ruling that leans on a scientific claim, or a bill whose sponsorship cites a study, currently sit in separate categories with no typed relation — connecting them would let a reader ask not just "is this claim settled" but *"is the thing this ruling was built on still standing?"*

And the endgame imposes unglamorous obligations first. The more interesting user of this system is not a person browsing but other tools querying it — an AI assistant or search engine that cites a claim's actual current status instead of returning a flattened answer. That requires server-rendered claim pages, per-receipt metadata, a sitemap, a public read API; an internal gap audit calls this the vision-blocking item, which is the correct kind of self-criticism for a project like this to publish.

## The wager

Every design decision above is the same decision in different clothes: when the record documents motion, record it with a receipt; when it doesn't, have the discipline to draw a flat line. The corpus's most common curve is a single honest point, and its most valuable property is that nobody — not a bulk SQL wave, not a language model, not the marketing copy — is allowed to bend that line without a document in hand.

The system runs at [epistemic-receipts.vercel.app](https://epistemic-receipts.vercel.app), corrections log included.
