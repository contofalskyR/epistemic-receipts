## Six months after the United States captured a country, look who's running its oil money

On January 3, everyone watched. U.S. forces struck Caracas, captured Nicolás Maduro, and flew him to New York to face charges. For about a week it was the only story in the world.

Then the cameras left. Here is what they left behind.

Venezuela is now run by an interim government under Maduro's former vice president — a kind of *Madurismo without Maduro*, operating under direct U.S. oversight while a "transition" is arranged. The most consequential part isn't the politics, though. It's the money. There has been roughly **$8 billion in Venezuelan oil sales this year — and only about $5.5 million of it has actually reached Venezuela.** The rest sits in U.S.-controlled accounts that even sympathetic analysts describe as opaque. Alongside it, new hydrocarbons and mining laws have been written to open Venezuela's mineral-rich Orinoco belt to foreign companies.

The politics haven't stopped either — as recently as mid-June, the National Assembly's leadership was meeting opposition figures over the makeup of the country's electoral council. Meanwhile, ordinary Venezuelans' expectations of a post-Maduro windfall have largely gone unmet, and the opposition's María Corina Machado — this year's Nobel Peace laureate — is still outside the country.

None of this is hidden. It's just no longer *covered*. In January this was wall-to-wall; today it's the province of think tanks and trade publications, not the evening news. A foreign power is running a country and its oil revenue and rewriting its resource laws, and it's happening at a fraction of the attention the capture itself got. That's not a conspiracy. It's just how the attention cycle works — the drama gets the cameras, the aftermath gets silence. Which is the entire reason this newsletter exists.

## The status board

Where four immigration fights actually stand this month.

**🟠 ORPHANED — The 3.3-million-person green-card review nobody is tracking.**
After the November shooting in Washington, the administration ordered a re-examination of green cards held by nationals of 19 (later more) countries — reporting put roughly **3.3 million lawful permanent residents** under review. It led the news for a week. Since then: near silence on outcomes, while the review grinds on with no announced decision date and no court case forcing one.
*Last real movement: the January expansion of the processing hold to 39 countries. Still unresolved. Nobody followed up.*

**🔴 STALLED — The $100,000 H-1B fee is now a coin flip.**
One federal court in D.C. upheld the fee. On June 8, a second court struck it down and called it an unlawful tax — then stayed its own ruling four days later, so employers are still being charged while it all plays out.
*Waiting on: appeals in the D.C. and First Circuits; the Supreme Court will likely have the final word. A ruling either way isn't the end — it's appealable, and being appealed.*

**🟢 RESOLVED (with an asterisk) — The student-visa purge got reversed.**
The spring-2025 mass SEVIS terminations that abruptly hit roughly 4,700 international students were walked back after more than a hundred lawsuits and dozens of court orders.
*But:* the government replaced them with a new termination framework that remains in litigation, and quieter terminations have continued. Resolved on paper — the sequel is live.

**🔴 STALLED — Venezuelan TPS is in limbo (see above).**
Protections for the 2023 Venezuela TPS group were terminated after the Supreme Court cleared the way last fall, but the underlying case is still sitting at the Ninth Circuit.
*Not abandoned — waiting on a court that hasn't ruled. There's a difference, and it's the whole point.*
