# The long-term news cycle: sources, landscape, allies

Research report for the dropped-story tracker (phase 2 of epistemic-receipts.vercel.app). Researched 2026-07-08. Every load-bearing claim is cited; unlabeled statements are drawn directly from a cited source. Passages marked **[inference]** are this report's own reasoning, not sourced fact.

---

## Executive summary

**The core bet survives, but needs sharper guardrails than the brief assumed.** Nobody in the paid-tracking market (POLITICO Pro, Bloomberg Government, FiscalNote, LexisNexis/Westlaw) alerts on silence — they alert on activity, confirmed across five independent vendors, most directly by Congress.gov's own help text: *"You will not receive an email if no new information has been added to the item you are tracking."* And the three existing immigration-specific status trackers (Justice Action Center, the Stanford/Yale Immigration Policy Tracking Project, Just Security) all track formal legal/administrative posture only — none measure media-attention decay. The attention-decay-layered-on-government-status wedge is real and, as far as this research found, unclaimed.

**But immigration is a genuinely hard launch domain for this exact signal**, and the adversarial pass surfaced this as the single most important finding of the whole research pass. The domain is unusually dense with mechanisms that produce "coverage went quiet + technically still open" *without* anything being neglected: visa bulletin dates that retrogress and advance monthly with zero news coverage either way, Temporary Protected Status designations that auto-extend by statute when DHS simply does nothing, and — most consequentially — an EOIR administrative-closure practice that TRAC documents averaging **15 years of dormancy at the immigration-court level and 29 years at the BIA level**, treated by the entire immigration bar as routine, not newsworthy. A tool that flags "no docket activity in 90 days" naively will bury a rare, real catch (like the 2013 Gang of Eight bill dying from Boehner simply never scheduling a vote) under hundreds of false positives from cases that are dormant by design. This is fixable — with venue-aware dormancy floors, multi-feed corroboration, explicit quiet-resolution pre-filters, and a human-review gate on early ORPHANED flags — but it is not optional, and it should be built before, not after, the first public flag.

**Recommended data stack:** GDELT DOC 2.0 (`artList` + `timelinevol`) stays the coverage backbone as planned — free, no key, and its `theme:` filter captures most of GKG's clustering value without the BigQuery/bulk-data cost of the raw Events/GKG tables. Pair it with two activity feeds: **congress.gov** (bill status, 5,000 req/hr, stable) and **CourtListener/RECAP webhooks** (docket alerts, push-based so no polling is needed) as the primary pair, with **Federal Register** (no key required) as a strong, cheap third if engineering time allows. Skip commercial NewsAPI-class services for now — none offer a production-legal free tier, and their ToS language is uniformly ambiguous-to-hostile toward long-term public display of derived data.

**Landscape:** the closest thing to a working analog for the "still open" data model is ICG CrisisWatch (monthly status + trend arrows since 2003) — instructive but resourced at a scale (~150 staff, $24M+ fund) this project cannot match. The single best pitch-narrative asset found is that even ProPublica — a well-funded, Pulitzer-winning newsroom — has multiple trackers (Treatment Tracker, Recovery Tracker) that were silently discontinued with no reader-facing "this is now stale" signal. That is the gap this product exists to close, stated by the industry's own graveyard.

**Funnel:** given a cold start, zero brand trust in a politically sensitive domain, and a crowded field of better-branded status trackers, **embedding/licensing into an existing outlet's audience is the higher-leverage first move**, not building an owned list from zero. Documented (NYC immigration nonprofit newsroom, ~$2.5M budget, multi-channel including WhatsApp) and ILW.com's Immigration Daily (36,000+ subscribers, the largest distribution surface found) are the strongest partner candidates. Run a thin owned newsletter on Buttondown in parallel — near-zero cost, and Buttondown is the only platform with a genuinely production-ready draft-then-human-approve API — but treat it as the retention layer, not the primary growth engine, for year one.

**Buyer evidence:** $49/month sits at the low end of a real, populated price band for solo/small-org monitoring tools (Mention Solo and Awario Starter are both exactly $49/mo) — plausible as an easy "yes" price, though the harder problem is that the target buyer (a solo immigration attorney or small nonprofit) may currently pay **$0** for any dedicated tracking tool at all, relying on free Congress.gov/LegiScan alerts and AILA listservs. The sale is "start paying for something," not "switch from a $175/month incumbent."

## 2. Landscape map: who already works the long cycle

### Slow journalism — format models, not competitors
- **Delayed Gratification** (The Slow Journalism Company) — 15 years running, ~8,000 paying subscribers, ~£800k revenue (2024), profitable, reader-funded, ad-free. Its signature device — infographics revisiting and updating prior stories — is the closest existing editorial analog to orphan-flagging, but manual and quarterly, not a live database. ([A Media Operator](https://www.amediaoperator.com/analysis/delayed-gratification-slow-news-15-years-no-ads/))
- **Tortoise Media** — financially fragile: losses exceeding £20.3m since 2019, pivoting to podcasts and paid corporate events after acquiring *The Observer* in 2025. ([Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/media_business/tortoise-media-losses-2023/))

### Standing trackers — proof of demand, and a cautionary tale

|---|---|---|---|
| **ICG CrisisWatch** | 70+ active conflicts + ~50 standby | ~150 staff, funding includes a ~$24.1M "Securing the Future" fund (June 2025) | Monthly entries since 2003 with up/down trend arrows — the **closest existing analog** to a structured, timestamped, still-open-vs-resolved signal, though at a budget scale this project cannot match. ([CrisisWatch](https://www.crisisgroup.org/crisiswatch)) |
| **CFR Global Conflict Tracker** | ~30 conflicts of US interest | CPA staff, foundation-funded | Status reassessed monthly by CPA staff, filterable, but no explicit closed-out state — items drop when no longer "ongoing." ([Methodology](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/methodology)) |

Recovery Tracker: "no longer maintained." Even a top-tier, Pulitzer-winning newsroom lets trackers silently go stale with no reader-facing "orphaned" signal — exactly the gap this product targets. ([Treatment Tracker](https://projects.propublica.org/treatment/), [Recovery Tracker](https://projects.propublica.org/recovery/)) |

### Immigration-specific status trackers — the real competitive set (verified and corrected)
Three trackers already claim the "immigration policy/litigation status" niche directly. All three were re-verified in an adversarial follow-up pass specifically to check whether any of them already track *media attention* alongside legal status:

- **Justice Action Center — Immigration Litigation Tracker.** **Correction to initial research:** the tracker's own homepage now carries the banner "**[NOTE: Archived in 2025]**." JAC continues publishing litigation *narrative* posts on its main site, but the structured, live, per-case tracker tool itself is discontinued. ([litigationtracker.justiceactioncenter.org](https://litigationtracker.justiceactioncenter.org/)) Status field was docket stage only; a "news coverage" field existed but held press links as supporting documentation, not a quantitative attention metric.
- **Stanford/Yale Immigration Policy Tracking Project** (Prof. Lucas Guttentag) — confirmed active with entries into April 2026. Status taxonomy is purely formal/administrative (*Rescinded*, *In Litigation*, *In Regulatory Agenda*). No media-coverage or attention field. ([immpolicytracking.org](https://immpolicytracking.org/about/))
- **Just Security litigation tracker** — confirmed active, 500+ cases, entries through January 2026. Pure legal event log (filings, orders, consolidations). No attention dimension. ([Just Security](https://www.justsecurity.org/107087/tracker-litigation-legal-challenges-trump-administration/))
- **Law360 Immigration Policy Tracker** — paywalled, for-profit legal-trade-press product; audience is paying legal professionals, competitor in framing only.
- **TRAC** (Syracuse University) — closest standing adjacent tracker *in the launch vertical specifically*, but operates at the aggregate-statistical level (e.g., detention population counts), not individual falsifiable-story tracking. ([TRAC](https://tracreports.org/immigration/))

**Verdict: the gap holds, narrower than originally assumed.** Across all three direct status trackers, the data model is formal-legal-status-only; none layer in coverage volume or attention decay. **[inference]** This means the differentiated pitch should lead with "we watch whether the press is still paying attention, not just whether the docket moved" rather than "we track litigation status," since the latter framing walks straight into three existing, better-resourced, better-branded competitors.

### "Whatever happened to..." formats and Wikipedia
A named example exists (*Global News What Happened To...?* podcast, Curiouscast) but the format is thinner than a named institution — mostly generic craft advice, not a flagship product. ([Curiouscast](https://curiouscast.ca/podcast/822/whatever-happened-to/)) Wikipedia's own current-events maintenance documentation explicitly names this exact failure mode as an acknowledged, unsolved risk: editors "begin a news article and then simply lose interest in the topic, whereupon the article becomes inaccurate," with no dedicated tooling found for flagging it. ([How the Current Events page works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:How_the_Current_events_page_works)) A second strong "even a huge, well-organized effort has this exact gap" data point.

### Academic frame — citable foundation
- **Anthony Downs, "Up and Down with Ecology: The 'Issue-Attention' Cycle,"** *The Public Interest*, Vol. 28 (1972) — the theoretical ancestor: five stages from pre-problem to post-problem, decay is structural and largely decoupled from whether the problem was actually solved. ([UNC PDF](https://fbaum.unc.edu/teaching/articles/Downs_Public_Interest_1972.pdf))
- **Soufan, M., "Measuring the Gap Between Media Coverage and Public Information Demand: Evidence from the 2026 Lebanon Conflict,"** arXiv:2604.16417 (2026) — **verified real** (exact title/author/abstract match on arxiv.org). GDELT + Google Trends, 11,623 articles: conflict coverage was 94.9% of news but only 36.9% of search interest; Economy/Living Conditions/Emigration made up 63.1% of search demand but 5.1% of coverage. **The single strongest available citation for the founder's thesis** — it directly quantifies the gap between what gets covered and what people want followed up on, using the same GDELT infrastructure this product plans to use. ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16417))
- **Brannon, W. & Roy, D., "The speed of news in Twitter (X) versus radio,"** *Scientific Reports* 14, 11939 (2024) — 517,000 hours of radio + 26.6M tweets across 1,694 news events: online news circulates and fades faster than radio. ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-61921-7))
- **Bros, Dufraisse, Popescu & Gatica-Perez, ICWSM 2026 / arXiv:2603.21519** — 1.7M Swiss news articles, change-point detection across story types — the closest published methodology to an automated story-lifecycle/death detector, and a plausible technical toolkit or collaboration lead. ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.21519))
- **Gap found:** abundant research exists on attention *decay* and *speed*, but nothing frames indefinitely-stalled, never-resolved stories as a distinct phenomenon from ordinary decay. **[inference]** That terminology gap is real white space a founder-researcher could stake out with a named construct, using Downs as ancestor and Soufan/Brannon-Roy as empirical scaffolding — a genuine PhD-adjacent angle, not just a marketing hook.

## 5. Who pays for tracking today

**Confirmed: existing paid tools alert on activity, never on silence** — consistent across five independent vendors in two adjacent verticals (public affairs + legal):

- **POLITICO Pro** — SMB avg. $35,685/yr, enterprise avg. $84,948/yr; "Legislative and Regulatory Compasses" alert on tracked-item progress, no dormancy alert type found. ([SpendHound](https://www.spendhound.com/marketplace/politico-pro-pricing))
- **Bloomberg Government** — typically $7,500–$14,000/yr; alerts explicitly designed to reduce alert *volume*, never to surface inactivity.
- **FiscalNote** — **financially distressed in 2026**: NYSE-delisted March 25, 2026 for trading below $1/share for 30 days, moved to OTC (~$0.17/share), announced a 25% workforce cut. Its own blog post on "what makes a good legislative tracker" frames quality entirely around coverage-completeness and alert-noise-reduction — never dormancy detection, a notable self-authored omission. ([FiscalNote blog](https://fiscalnote.com/blog/silent-cost-legislation-tracker), [SEC 8-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1823466/000119312526124523/d31744dex991.htm))
- **LexisNexis / Westlaw (CourtLink)** — docket alerts fire only on new filings; solo/small-firm pricing runs $114–300+/month.
- **The single most direct piece of evidence:** Congress.gov's own help documentation states plainly: *"You will not receive an email if no new information has been added to the item you are tracking."* ([congress.gov/help/alerts](https://www.congress.gov/help/alerts)) The reference tool underlying most commercial trackers is architecturally activity-only by design.

**Down-market willingness-to-pay:** $49/month sits inside a real, populated price band for solo/small-org monitoring tools — Mention "Solo" and Awario "Starter" are both exactly $49/month. It's well above what individual immigration attorneys already pay for professional-association access (AILA nonprofit dues ~$13.50/month) and cheap policy newsletters ($10/month), and well below adjacent single-purpose tools (immigration case-management software $159/month, solo legal research $114–152/month). **Verdict: plausible but not proven** — the harder problem than pricing is that the target buyer may currently pay **$0** for any dedicated tracking tool, relying on free Congress.gov/LegiScan alerts and AILA listservs; the sale is "start paying," not "switch from a pricier incumbent."

## 6. Newsletter mechanics (funnel, light)

## 7. Adversarial pass: is the orphan signal noise?

This section directly executes the brief's required adversarial checkpoint — steelmanning the position that "coverage went quiet + formally unresolved" is not a meaningful signal.

**The attack lands, hard, in this specific domain.** Immigration is unusually dense with mechanisms that produce exactly the "silent + technically unresolved" pattern for entirely benign reasons:

1. **Visa bulletin retrogression/advancement** resolves and reverses essentially every month through a routine State Department process that generates no news coverage in either direction — a naive reading would flag constant false "orphans." ([Fragomen, July 2026 Visa Bulletin](https://www.fragomen.com/insights/united-states-july-2026-visa-bulletin-slight-retrogression-for-eb-1-india-advancement-for-most-other-employment-based-categories-uscis-to-honor-employment-based-final-action-dates.html))
2. **TPS auto-extension by statutory default** — if DHS doesn't publish a determination 60 days before expiry, protection auto-extends 6 months. A government-feed reader sees "no final determination" (looks OPEN) when the practical outcome is already locked in. ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/29/g-s1-130942/temporary-protected-status-program-explainer-supreme-court))
3. **Administrative closure — the single biggest false-positive trap.** TRAC/EOIR data shows the *average* administratively closed case sits dormant **~15 years at the immigration-court level, ~29 years at the BIA level** — a deliberate, well-understood parking mechanism the entire immigration bar treats as routine. ([TRAC](https://tracreports.org/immigration/reports/623/))
4. **Ordinary litigation dormancy is long by default**, not exceptional — federal appellate opinions can take 18+ months after full briefing with "no fixed deadline," explicitly described by the ABA as "part of how appellate courts operate," and EOIR's own backlog (3.6M cases in FY2024) makes months-to-years of case-level silence the norm, not a signal. ([ABA](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/litigation/resources/newsletters/appellate-practice/spring2024-steady-slow-understanding-navigating-delay-federal-courts/), [CRS R47077](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47077))
5. **Story reframing without keyword continuity** — "zero tolerance" coverage didn't stop, it continued as "Title 42," then "Title 8 asylum restrictions." A keyword/entity-matched tracker watching the original term sees false silence. ([Migration Policy Institute](https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/title-42-autopsy))
6. **Quiet settlements are real resolutions** that draw a fraction of the filing's press coverage and can take months to formally close on the docket — coverage-decay-plus-technically-open-docket is exactly the false-positive window.
7. **GDELT itself has a documented accuracy problem independent of the domain**: a 2025 peer-reviewed study found GDELT's key-field accuracy at only ~55%; the UK Office for National Statistics formally distrusts GDELT as a primary signal; a separate study found only 21% of GDELT-flagged protest-event URLs actually covered a real protest (79% false-positive rate on event extraction). ([MDPI/Data](https://www.mdpi.com/2306-5729/10/10/158), [ONS](https://www.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/birthsdeathsandmarriages/deaths/methodologies/globaldatabaseofeventslanguageandtonegdeltdataqualitynote))
8. **Attention decay is the statistical default, not the exception** — median major-story lifespan is ~7 days, article engagement half-life ~2.6 days. Any silence threshold under a few weeks will fire on nearly every story. This is exactly Downs' point: attention "is likely to wane before it is known whether the policy has actually mitigated the problem" — decay happens regardless of resolution.

**But the signal isn't pure noise, either — the counter-example that keeps the premise honest:** the 2013 "Gang of Eight" immigration reform bill passed the Senate 68–32, then died purely because Speaker Boehner never scheduled a House floor vote. "No vote" *was* the accountability story, and it was substantively newsworthy — silence-plus-inaction is exactly the pattern the tool exists to catch. ([Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-collapse-of-bipartisan-immigration-reform-a-guide-for-the-perplexed/))

**What this implies for thresholds:**
- **A grace period before the silence clock starts** — roughly 30–45 days after peak coverage volume, since ordinary story decay happens within the first ~1–3 weeks regardless of resolution.
- **Venue-calibrated dormancy floors, not a flat number** — court-docket silence under ~9–12 months shouldn't register as unusual at all, and administratively closed cases or cases under an active stay order should be excluded from the dormancy clock entirely (detect the order type, not just "no new filing").
- **Require corroboration from 2+ independent feeds** before an ORPHANED flag fires — e.g., docket silence *and* absence of a settlement/termination filing *and* no bill-text incorporation elsewhere (GovTrack found ~3% of "dead" bills had their text folded into other enacted legislation).
- **Pre-filter known quiet-resolution patterns into RESOLVED** before they reach the ORPHANED gate: TPS auto-extension logic, direct-final-rule effective dates with no adverse comments, settlement/stipulation-of-dismissal filings.
- **Publish and hold to a false-positive ceiling** before public launch, with a human-review gate on ORPHANED flags specifically during the early credibility-building period — borrowing the security-industry norm that a >20–30% false-positive rate signals detection logic that's structurally broken, not just noisy.

**Honest verdict:** the core premise — that coverage attention decays on a schedule largely decoupled from resolution, so cross-checking against a government-activity feed is a reasonable idea — survives. **The launch-domain choice does not survive unmodified.** **[inference]** Immigration combines the highest known density of statutory auto-resolution mechanisms (TPS, visa bulletin) with one of the longest normal-dormancy baselines in the federal system (EOIR backlog, multi-decade administrative closure) — both halves of the "quiet + unresolved" conjunction are individually noisy here, so their intersection does not self-clean. This is a build-order problem, not a fatal flaw: the pre-filters and corroboration rules above need to exist *before* the first public ORPHANED flag on an immigration story, or the tool risks a reputationally disproportionate debunking (e.g., "you flagged a case that was just in a routine discovery stay") in its very first weeks.
